Reflection

"Writing in the Digital Media" sounded like the methodology and approach of writing online journalism. In retrospect, my presumption couldn't have been further from the truth. Granted that's probably a slight overstatement, my mental compass was certainly pointing my expectations in the wrong direction when I arrived to the first day of class. Fortunately, this new direction revealed a more beautiful horizon: HTML.

Like the most new concepts to me, coding didn't sound too achievable or learnable. In fact, before this class, I thought coding was secluded for those ingenious hackers you see in the movies opening maximum-security doors from their bedroom closets.

However, I was pleasantly surprised to learn that coding HTML was extremely intuitive and understandable. Despite having only scratched the surface, I am already familiar with many of the tools and abilities that HTML provides. For example, before this class, I didn't know where to begin to create a webpage, let alone uploading a GIF. Now, I can perform these tasks with somewhat effortlessness. But don't be fooled by this impressively professional coding; there were many learning curves I had to overcome.

My greatest struggle in HTML thus far was learning how to input images. The function seemed to require a coding fluentness that that I was just beginning to speak. Before this, I had only created simple text on a naked, whitewashed webpage. To me, it was like attempting to have a full-on Spanish conversation with only knowing, "Me llamo Loren".



With the help of Lynda.com, the Internet, and class, this feature became second nature; now, I can't stop doing it! Another trial during this module was GitHub. Accompanied with Atom, this program seemed to add on one more too many moving parts to coding.

The modular readings have provided a new perspective and appreciation for what I'm doing in class. To know that I'm working with the very idea that Berners-Lee planted at CERN twenty-seven years ago is somewhat incredible –– how far technology has come is even more so.

Loren Skinker
